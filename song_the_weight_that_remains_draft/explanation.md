# The Weight That Remains

---

**A poem about what cannot be lost.**

It strips away the usual consolations—money, futures, even names—and asks what stays when all of that goes. The answer: what was forged in you by pressure, time, and expenditure. The poem doesn't offer hope. It offers something harder: the recognition that loss is not subtraction but *uncovering*.

**The core argument:**
- Loss appears as power leaving or possibilities dying—but both are disguises.
- Loneliness is not absence; it's the sound of your own voice unmasked.
- The forge (what shaped you) remembers what was spent and what remains.
- The wheel turns, but the weight is yours—you were *made* to contain it.
- The only move left: sit still in the losing. See what holds when nothing is choosing.

---

**Structure:** Circular, repetitive, tightening inward—each stanza a turn of the same wheel, each turn closer to the still center.

**Tone:** Sparse. Certain. Not warm, but not cruel. A voice that has sat through the fire and tells you: you can too.

**Final instruction:** Stop renaming, stop escaping, stop grasping. Sit. Hold. Remain.

---

*You cannot lose what you were made to contain.*
